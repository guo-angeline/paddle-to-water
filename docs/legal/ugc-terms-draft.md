# Part 2: Questions for the licensed attorney (prioritized)

Ordered by expensive-if-wrong. Each is a specific opinion request, not a general "review this."

1. **Liability cap against the wrongful-death / drowning backdrop (Section 10.4).** Highest stakes. Can a limitation-of-liability clause on a *free* site meaningfully cap exposure when the underlying activity carries death risk? Specifically: (a) what enforceable form and amount should the cap take when there are no fees paid; (b) how does it interact with the D25 assumption-of-risk waiver and the fact that a wrongful-death claim may belong independently to non-signatory heirs; (c) confirm the cap cannot and does not purport to waive gross negligence (City of Santa Barbara). Do not let the bracketed placeholder ship unexamined.

2. **Defamation exposure from hosting stranger reviews about NAMED PRIVATE BUSINESSES (Sections 5.2, 7.2, 8).** This feature invites authenticated strangers to write factual and opinion statements about real, named businesses (e.g., the 101 Surf Sports private dock, marinas, shops). Opine on: how much the opinion-vs-fact framing, the contributor representations (Section 4), the indemnity (Section 8), and the pre-moderation posture actually reduce the Site's own exposure; whether a public business-complaint/takedown path helps or hurts; and whether the Site should decline reviews of private businesses entirely versus manage them.

3. **Section 230 preservation (Sections 3.2, 6, 7).** Confirm the design choices, binary publish-or-reject, human pre-moderation, and no in-place editing of user text, preserve the Site's status as a provider of an interactive computer service under 47 U.S.C. Section 230, and that nothing in the moderation flow (including any redaction) rises to "material contribution" that forfeits the shield. Confirm the IP carve-out to Section 230 is fully backstopped by the DMCA path and a registered designated agent.

4. **Interlock with the existing safety disclaimer (Section 10.1).** Confirm this UGC document and the live `/disclaimer` page plus the "Guidance only, not a safety guarantee" line reinforce rather than contradict, and that introducing user ratings/aggregates does not create an implied representation of safety that undercuts either the disclaimer or the D25 waiver. Flag any wording in either document that a plaintiff's lawyer could use to argue the Site vouched for a spot's safety.

5. **CCPA/CPRA and the required-sign-in PII surface (Sections 2.2, 9).** Required Google sign-in turns a static site into a personal-data processor. Opine on: which CCPA/CPRA obligations are triggered (applicability threshold vs. voluntary compliance), the correct do-not-sell/share and rights disclosures, the identity-provider data-sharing disclosure, and how a deletion request reconciles with (a) retained moderation/legal-defense copies and (b) a published review that is also protected user speech.

6. **Assent mechanism and enforceability (Sections 1.2, 11.1).** Given the heightened liability surface, advise whether sign-in-wrap/action-based assent is enough or whether an affirmative "I agree" step at submission (and re-assent on material change) is needed for these terms and the liability cap to bind.

7. **Arbitration / class-waiver and choice-of-law/venue (Section 12).** A strategic call for a solo California operator: whether binding arbitration plus a class-action waiver is net-positive here (including mass-arbitration cost exposure), and the governing-law/venue set.

8. **Indemnity enforceability (Section 8).** Confirm a broad consumer-facing indemnity survives an unconscionability challenge under California law, or narrow it. Note its practical value is deterrence and defense-cost allocation, not collection.

9. **FTC fake-review rule alignment (Sections 2.4, 4.4).** Confirm the real-experience representations plus one-review-per-account moderation adequately support compliance with 16 CFR Part 465, and whether a material-connection disclosure clause should be added for contributors tied to a reviewed business.
